1. What "Conformal Cooling Tooling" Means

In tooling procurement, the phrase "conformal cooling" is applied to the channels — not the mold as a whole. Conformal cooling tooling means any mold tooling component that has internally printed cooling channels manufactured by LPBF (Laser Powder Bed Fusion) metal 3D printing, where those channels follow the contour of the mold cavity surface at a constant offset distance (typically 8–15mm).

Critically, the term covers a wide range of individual components. You do not need to replace an entire mold to purchase conformal cooling tooling:

  • Insert — a removable block that forms part of the cavity or core surface; the most common conformal cooling tooling purchase
  • Core pin — a cylindrical component forming an internal feature; conformal channels spiral around the core axis instead of the impractical alternative of a bubbler
  • Cavity block — the full cavity-side forming component of a mold, printed entirely in AM with integrated conformal channels
  • Sprue bushing — the gate entry component; a conformal sprue bushing eliminates gate blush and reduces sprue cooling time by 40–60%
  • Slide — the moving side-action component; slides are often the last cooling bottleneck in a well-designed mold
  • Full mold core/cavity set — all forming components printed with integrated conformal channels; highest investment, maximum thermal performance

What conformal cooling tooling is not: it is not a "bubbler and baffle" upgrade, not a better-positioned conventional drill pattern, and not a label that can be applied to EDM-machined channels in a mold base. The defining manufacturing process is additive — LPBF printing creates channel geometries that no subtractive process can replicate.

Procurement shortcut: When a supplier quotes "conformal cooling," always ask: "Is the channel geometry created by LPBF printing, or by another process?" Only LPBF-printed channels can truly conform to arbitrary surface geometry. Any other answer means the product is not conformal cooling tooling by the technical definition.

3. What to Specify in Your RFQ (10-Item Checklist)

The quality of a conformal cooling tooling quote is directly proportional to the completeness of the RFQ you send. Vague enquiries produce vague quotes with wide price ranges and unworkable timelines. Send this 10-item checklist with every RFQ:

  • 1
    Part geometry file STEP or IGES preferred. STEP (AP214 or AP242) is the universal standard — it carries solid geometry the supplier can import directly into simulation software. If you can only share 2D drawings due to NDA constraints, include all critical dimensions, draft angles, and wall thickness map.
  • 2
    Mold base standard and interface dimensions State the mold base supplier (DME, HASCO, LKM, etc.) and standard (e.g., "HASCO K-plate, 346×296mm"). Include the insert pocket dimensions (length × width × depth) and positional tolerances. This is non-negotiable for a retrofit insert — the tooling must drop into your existing pocket without modification.
  • 3
    Coolant port size and thread standard Specify the inlet/outlet port diameter and thread: BSP (British Standard Pipe), NPT (National Pipe Thread), or metric. Include port position preference if you have one. Most Chinese suppliers default to G3/8" BSP — flag if you need NPT for US-market mold bases.
  • 4
    Working pressure State your mold cooling system's operating pressure in bar or PSI. This determines the wall thickness required between channels and the cavity surface (the pressure-to-wall-thickness safety factor). Standard range is 6–10 bar for water cooling. Every MouldNova insert is hydrostatic-tested at 1.5× your stated working pressure before shipping.
  • 5
    Coolant type Water (standard) or thermal oil (used in some high-temperature applications). Coolant type affects material selection — oil cooling at elevated temperatures rules out some material options and changes seal design. Also specify any corrosion-inhibitor additives you use, which affects stainless steel grade selection.
  • 6
    Surface finish requirement State the cavity face finish requirement in SPI classification (A1 mirror through D3 matte) or Ra value in µm. Surface finish drives post-processing time and cost more than almost any other single factor. A mirror-polish SPI A1 finish on a complex surface can add 3–5 days and $300–800 to an insert cost versus a standard EDM or machined finish.
  • 7
    Material preference or performance requirement If you have a specific material requirement, state it. If not, describe your application constraints: injection pressure, material being moulded (PP, ABS, PA66-GF30, etc.), required hardness, and whether corrosion resistance is needed. The supplier can then recommend the optimal material — 420SS, 18Ni300, H13, or CuCrZr.
  • 8
    Quantity required and repeat order forecast State how many pieces you need now and whether this is a one-time purchase or will repeat. Higher quantities may qualify for pricing breaks. More importantly, multi-cavity molds require matched inserts — confirm the number of identical inserts needed and whether they must be from the same print batch (important for dimensional consistency).
  • 9
    Inspection and certification requirements Specify what inspection documentation you need: CMM dimensional report, hardness certificate, channel pressure test certificate, material mill certificate, Ra surface roughness measurement. MouldNova includes CMM report + pressure test certificate as standard on every order; specify any additional requirements in your RFQ so they are priced in from the start.
  • 10
    NDA requirement If sharing proprietary part geometry, confirm whether you need an NDA signed before file transfer. Most professional conformal cooling tooling suppliers will sign an NDA on request. Include this requirement in your first email — it signals you are a serious buyer and avoids delays once you are ready to share files.

4. Material Selection Guide

Material selection for conformal cooling tooling is a balance between thermal conductivity, mechanical strength, corrosion resistance, and cost. The four LPBF-printable materials used in production tooling each have a distinct sweet spot:

Material Hardness (after HT) Thermal Conductivity Cost Index Best For
420 Stainless Steel HRC 50–52 ~25 W/m·K 1.0× (baseline) Standard injection molding; good corrosion resistance; balanced cost and performance; most common choice for PP, ABS, PE parts
18Ni300 Maraging Steel HRC 52–54 ~20 W/m·K 1.3× High-strength applications; thin walls or aggressive part geometry under high injection pressure; PA66-GF, POM, high-performance engineering plastics
H13 Tool Steel HRC 44–48 ~28 W/m·K 1.1× Die casting tooling; elevated operating temperature (>200°C); aluminium and zinc die casting; also suitable for high-volume injection molding above 1M shots
CuCrZr (Copper Alloy) HRC 28–32 ~320 W/m·K 1.7–1.9× Maximum heat removal; gate zone inserts; thick wall sections; optical-grade parts; medical devices where cycle time is critical and hardness requirements are lower

Material decision logic

Default to 420SS unless you have a specific reason to upgrade. It offers good corrosion resistance (water cooling safe), is the most widely manufactured conformal material, and is the most cost-effective for most production scenarios.

Upgrade to 18Ni300 when: injection pressure exceeds 1,400 bar, wall thickness at any point on the insert is below 3mm, or the moulded material has high glass fill content generating elevated shear forces.

Select H13 for die casting tooling or when the mold operates above 180°C on a regular basis. H13 has better red hardness (hot hardness) than 420SS or 18Ni300.

Select CuCrZr when the thermal bottleneck is severe and no steel can provide sufficient heat flux. Typical use cases: gate inserts on thick-walled parts, core pins on very deep features (aspect ratio >5:1), optical or medical parts where minimal cycle time variation matters more than insert longevity.

Hardness note: CuCrZr at HRC 28–32 will wear faster than steel in abrasive conditions. If you are moulding glass-filled, mineral-filled, or flame-retardant compounds, CuCrZr may not be appropriate for the cavity face — consider a hybrid design where CuCrZr handles the cooling geometry but a steel wear face is bonded to the cavity surface.

5. How Conformal Cooling Tooling Is Priced

Three factors drive the price of a conformal cooling tooling order, in order of influence:

Cost Driver 1: Geometry Complexity

Channel routing complexity is the primary cost driver. A simple cylindrical core pin with a helical spiral channel is straightforward to design and print. A cavity insert with five independent parallel circuits, each following compound-curve surface geometry and routing around 12 ejector pin locations, requires significantly more engineering time and careful print orientation to achieve adequate surface quality on all channel walls. Geometry complexity drives both the design hours billed and the print time (more support structure = more post-processing).

Cost Driver 2: Material

LPBF powder costs vary significantly by alloy. At current market pricing from China-based suppliers:

  • 420SS powder: ~$40–60/kg
  • H13 tool steel powder: ~$55–80/kg
  • 18Ni300 maraging steel powder: ~$80–120/kg
  • CuCrZr copper alloy powder: ~$180–280/kg

A 1.5kg insert therefore has a material cost of $60–90 in 420SS versus $270–420 in CuCrZr — before any processing. Material choice visibly impacts final price, particularly for larger components.

Cost Driver 3: Post-Processing

LPBF printing produces a near-net-shape component that requires significant post-processing before it is a finished tooling insert. Post-processing typically includes: stress relief anneal, wire EDM or CNC to remove the build plate, rough CNC machining of all mating faces, vacuum heat treatment to final hardness, finish CNC on precision faces, manual polishing of the cavity surface, and final inspection. On a typical $2,500 insert, post-processing represents 45–55% of total cost. This is the most commonly under-quoted stage by less experienced suppliers — always confirm that heat treatment and final hardness testing are included.

7. Red Flags When Evaluating Suppliers

Five behaviours in supplier conversations that indicate serious competence or quality gaps:

🚩

No hydrostatic pressure test certificate offered

Every conformal cooling insert must be pressure-tested at 1.5× working pressure before shipping. A blocked channel or micro-crack that leaks under production conditions causes catastrophic mold damage. If a supplier doesn't mention pressure testing or treats it as an "optional extra," they have either not encountered this failure mode or have decided to ignore it. Require a dated, signed pressure test certificate — zero-drop at 1.5× working pressure for 10 minutes — as a standard delivery document.

🚩

No Moldflow simulation capability

A supplier who cannot show you Moldflow simulation results for your specific part — before manufacturing — is asking you to buy on faith. Any claimed cycle time reduction ("we'll give you 35% improvement") that isn't backed by simulation data for your geometry is marketing copy, not engineering. Simulation is the minimum credibility threshold. MouldNova provides this free before any order commitment.

🚩

No ISO 9001 certification

ISO 9001 does not guarantee quality, but its absence in a precision tooling supplier means there is no formal quality management system governing incoming material inspection, process control, or outgoing inspection. For mold tooling that must fit to ±0.02mm tolerances and operate at hundreds of bar of injection pressure, process discipline matters. Request the certificate number and confirm expiry date — expired certificates are a common shortcut.

🚩

Cannot provide Ra measurement on sealing faces

The parting line and mating faces of a conformal cooling insert are critical sealing surfaces. If a supplier cannot provide a measured Ra value for these surfaces and a specified tolerance, it means they are not inspecting them — and there is no guarantee the insert will seal correctly at pressure when fitted into your mold base. Ask: "What is the Ra specification on the mating flange, and can you show the measurement in the inspection report?" A non-answer is a red flag.

🚩

Vague or evasive about channel geometry verification

You cannot directly inspect internal LPBF-printed channels by visual inspection or conventional CMM. Professional suppliers verify channel geometry through: (a) process validation — confirming the print file matches the design CAD, (b) pressure testing at 1.5× working pressure confirming channel integrity and absence of blockage, and (c) for complex geometries, X-ray CT scanning or borescope inspection. A supplier who says "we can see it's fine" or "we trust our process" without offering a formal verification method is leaving you exposed to in-service failure.

When comparing quotations from multiple conformal cooling tooling suppliers, use this 5-point scorecard. Score each supplier 0–2 on each point (0 = absent, 1 = partial, 2 = complete). Maximum score: 10.

Lead time accuracy and commitment

3

Simulation included or offered

Is Moldflow simulation (or equivalent thermal FEA) included in the quotation, or offered as a step before manufacturing?

Score 2: Simulation results for your part included in the quote package, or explicitly offered free before order confirmation.
Score 0: No mention of simulation; cycle time claims made without supporting data.
4

References or case study data available

Can the supplier provide real project data — cycle time reduction achieved, client industry, tooling type — for a comparable application?

Score 2: Provides 2+ case studies with before/after cycle time data for comparable part geometry or material.
Score 0: "We have many satisfied customers" with no data, or case studies with no measurable outcomes.
5

Terms and conditions clarity

Are payment terms, warranty, liability on dimensional non-conformance, and shipping terms (EXW, FOB, DDP) clearly stated?

Score 2: Full T&Cs included; DDP shipping specified; warranty period and dimensional rectification terms stated.
Score 0: Price only; no T&Cs; shipping terms unstated; no mention of what happens if the insert doesn't fit.
Interpretation: Score 8–10: Strong supplier, proceed with order. Score 5–7: Acceptable, but clarify scoring gaps before committing. Score below 5: Significant risk — do not commit to a tooling order without resolving the gaps, particularly if inspection documentation and lead time commitment are the scoring failures.

The 100,000 shots/year threshold: Based on our production data across customer projects, conformal cooling tooling almost always delivers a positive 3-year TCO versus conventional tooling when annual volume exceeds 100,000 shots and the part has at least one thermal bottleneck zone identifiable in Moldflow. Below 50,000 shots/year, the payback period typically exceeds 18 months and the case depends heavily on defect reduction value rather than machine time savings alone.

Additional TCO factors not captured in the table above, but consistently observed in production:

  • Fewer trial rounds (T-trials): Conformal cooling molds designed with pre-validated Moldflow simulation typically pass T1 or T2. Conventional molds often require T3–T4 due to hot spot-driven warpage discovered only in production. Each additional trial round costs $2,000–8,000 in machine time, material, and engineering.
  • Extended tool life: More uniform thermal cycling means less thermally-induced fatigue crack formation at stress concentration points. Consistent data from our customers shows conformal tooling reaching 500k–1M shots with no cracking versus conventional tools at the same shot count often showing early-stage fatigue cracks at deep ribs or bosses.
  • Energy cost reduction: Shorter cycle time at the same output = fewer machine operating hours = lower energy consumption. At 150k shots/year and 583 fewer hours running, this represents a measurable reduction in electricity costs that compounds annually.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is conformal cooling tooling?
Conformal cooling tooling refers to any mold tooling component — insert, core pin, cavity block, sprue bushing, or slide — that has internally printed cooling channels manufactured by LPBF (Laser Powder Bed Fusion) metal 3D printing. The channels follow the shape of the mold cavity surface at a constant 8–15mm offset, enabling uniform heat extraction that is physically impossible to achieve with conventionally drilled straight channels. The term covers both retrofit inserts (added to an existing mold) and newly designed components with conformal channels integrated from the start.
How much does conformal cooling tooling cost?
Cost depends on tooling type and complexity. Simple conformal inserts (≤100×100×80mm, 420SS, standard channel pattern) cost $800–2,500. Complex inserts (multi-zone routing, 18Ni300, large footprint) run $2,500–8,000. Full conformal molds incorporating multiple tooling components cost $15,000–80,000+. These are China (Ningbo) pricing tiers — European and North American suppliers typically charge 2.2–2.8× these figures. What is the typical lead time for conformal cooling tooling?
MouldNova's standard lead time for conformal cooling inserts is 7–12 working days from design approval to DDP delivery. Breakdown: design review 1–2 days, LPBF printing 2–4 days, post-processing (stress relief, CNC, heat treatment, polishing) 2–3 days, QC and pressure test plus shipping preparation 2–3 days. Full conformal molds (new build including mold base) take 35–55 working days. A committed ship date is provided in every quotation.
What material should I specify for conformal cooling tooling?
The four main LPBF-printable options are: 420 Stainless Steel (HRC 50–52) — default for most injection molding applications; 18Ni300 Maraging Steel (HRC 52–54) — upgrade for high-injection-pressure or thin-wall applications; H13 Tool Steel (HRC 44–48) — preferred for die casting and high operating temperatures; CuCrZr copper alloy (HRC 28–32, thermal conductivity 320 W/m·K) — when maximum heat removal rate is the priority, such as gate zone inserts or thick wall sections. For most standard injection molding, 420SS is the correct default. Specify your application conditions (injection pressure, moulded material, expected mold temperature) and your supplier should recommend the appropriate material.
What should I include in an RFQ for conformal cooling tooling?
A complete RFQ should include: (1) Part geometry file (STEP preferred); (2) Mold base standard and insert pocket dimensions; (3) Coolant port size and thread standard (BSP or NPT); (4) Working pressure in bar or PSI; (5) Coolant type (water or oil); (6) Surface finish requirement (SPI grade or Ra value); (7) Material preference or application constraints; (8) Quantity required and repeat order forecast; (9) Inspection requirements (CMM, pressure test cert, hardness cert, material cert); (10) NDA requirement if sharing proprietary geometry. Suppliers who receive a complete RFQ respond faster and more accurately than those receiving an incomplete enquiry.
