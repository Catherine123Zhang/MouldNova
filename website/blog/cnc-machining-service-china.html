2. Types of CNC Machining Services Available in China

A capable CNC machining service in China offers more than just milling and turning. Here is a breakdown of the core processes and what to look for in each.

CNC Milling: 3-Axis, 4-Axis, and 5-Axis

3-axis CNC milling is the workhorse process for prismatic parts — brackets, housings, plates, and fixtures. Virtually every Chinese CNC shop offers 3-axis capability. Machine brands to look for: Mazak VCN, Haas VF series, or domestic equivalents with Fanuc or Siemens controls.

4-axis CNC milling adds a rotary axis, enabling machining of features on multiple faces in a single setup. This reduces setup time and improves positional accuracy between features. Common applications: manifold blocks, valve bodies, and multi-port hydraulic components.

5-axis CNC milling is the benchmark for complex geometry: impellers, turbine blades, aerospace structural components, and mold cavities with deep ribs and undercuts. Top-tier Chinese shops run 5-axis machines from DMG MORI, Makino, and Hermle. Shop rates for 5-axis work in China run $30–45/hr — versus $120–250/hr in Germany.

CNC Turning and Swiss Turning

CNC turning produces cylindrical parts: shafts, bushings, sleeves, pins, and threaded components. Look for shops running Mazak Quick Turn or Okuma LB series lathes with live tooling capability — this allows milling features (flats, cross-holes, keyways) on the lathe without a second setup.

Swiss-type CNC turning (also called sliding-headstock turning) is the process of choice for small, long, precision parts: medical bone screws, electrical connector pins, watch components, and nozzle tips. The sliding headstock design provides exceptional rigidity and accuracy on parts with length-to-diameter ratios of 3:1 and beyond.

At Saiguang's Yuyao facility, we run the TSUGAMI M08J-II Swiss-type lathe for precision turned parts that require tolerances tighter than ±0.01mm. This machine handles bar stock up to 20mm diameter with 8-axis simultaneous control, producing complex geometries in a single cycle — including turning, milling, drilling, and thread-whirling.

Wire EDM and Sinker EDM

Wire EDM (Electrical Discharge Machining) cuts conductive materials with a thin wire electrode, achieving tolerances of ±0.003mm and surface finishes to Ra 0.2µm. Primary applications: mold cavity inserts, stamping dies, precision gears, and any part requiring sharp internal corners that a milling cutter cannot reach.

Sinker EDM (also called die-sinking EDM or ram EDM) uses a shaped electrode to erode a mirror-image cavity into the workpiece. This is the standard process for injection mold cavities, rib details, micro-features, and textured surfaces.

Our Charmilles ROBOFORM 35 sinker EDM handles mold cavities up to 350 × 250mm with positioning accuracy of ±0.002mm. The machine's automatic tool changer runs unattended overnight, significantly reducing lead times for complex mold components. For a deeper look at our EDM and CNC capabilities, see our CNC / EDM / Injection Molding service page.

Surface Finishing and Secondary Operations

A full-service CNC machining supplier in China should offer the following secondary operations either in-house or through verified local partners:

  • Anodizing: Type II (decorative, 5–25µm) and Type III hard anodize (25–75µm) for aluminum parts
  • Electroless nickel plating: 10–50µm coating for corrosion resistance and hardness (HV 500–700 after heat treatment)
  • Chrome plating: Decorative or hard chrome (HV 850–1050) for wear surfaces
  • Passivation: Citric acid or nitric acid passivation for stainless steel parts per ASTM A967
  • Heat treatment: Vacuum hardening, nitriding, carburizing, stress relieving, and age hardening
  • Polishing: From standard machined finish (Ra 1.6µm) to mirror polish (Ra 0.05µm)
  • Grinding: Surface grinding (±0.005mm flatness), cylindrical grinding, and centerless grinding
  • Laser marking/engraving: Part numbers, logos, and traceability codes

Hidden Costs to Factor In

The quoted part price from a Chinese CNC supplier is not your total cost. Make sure you account for:

  4. Wire transfer fees: International T/T transfers cost $25–50 per transaction. For small orders, this can add 1–2%.
  5. Packaging and customs brokerage: Export-ready packaging adds $0.50–3.00 per part. US customs brokerage costs $150–250 per shipment.
  6. Communication and project management time: Your engineering and procurement team's time managing an overseas supplier has a real cost. A well-run supplier minimizes this; a poorly run one multiplies it.

4. How to Choose a Reliable CNC Supplier in China

The difference between a good and bad experience sourcing CNC machining services in China comes down to supplier selection. Follow this systematic approach.

Certifications to Verify

  • ISO 9001:2015 — Minimum standard for any supplier. Verify the certificate is current and issued by an accredited registrar (UKAS, CNAS, or equivalent).
  • IATF 16949 — Required for automotive components. Demonstrates APQP, PPAP, and FMEA capability.
  • AS9100D — Required for aerospace components. Verifies traceability, FOD prevention, and special process control.
  • ISO 13485 — Required for medical device components. Covers risk management and design control.
  • ISO 14001 — Environmental management. Increasingly required by European OEMs.

Equipment Brands to Ask About

The machines on a shop floor tell you more than any sales pitch. Here is what to look for:

Tier Machine Brands What It Tells You
Tier 1 (Premium) Mazak, Okuma, Makino, DMG MORI, Hermle, TSUGAMI, GF/Charmilles, Sodick High-precision shop, serious investment, capable of tight tolerances and complex geometry
Tier 2 (Good) Haas, Doosan, Hyundai, Brother, Tongtai, Victor Taichung, Mitsubishi Solid production capability, good value, suitable for most commercial machining work
Tier 3 (Budget) Chinese domestic brands (Shenyang, Dalian, Bochi), older refurbished machines Cost-focused shop, acceptable for simple parts with standard tolerances (±0.05mm+)

10-Point Supplier Audit Checklist

1
Business license verification: Confirm the company is registered as a manufacturer (not a trading company) through China's National Enterprise Credit Information Publicity System (NECIPS).
2
Machine list with serial numbers: Request a complete equipment list including brand, model, year of manufacture, and CNC control system. Verify key machines during a video or in-person tour.
3
Inspection equipment audit: Verify they own a CMM (Zeiss, Hexagon, or Mitutoyo), profilometer, hardness tester, and calibrated hand tools. Ask for calibration certificates.
4
Material certificates: Request sample mill certificates (MTR/MTC) for materials they commonly use. Verify they source from certified steel mills and distributors.
5
Sample parts: Order 3–5 sample parts in your actual material before committing to production. Measure them yourself or send to a third-party metrology lab.
6
Reference customers: Ask for 2–3 references from companies in your industry. Contact them directly and ask about quality, delivery, and communication.
7
Process flow documentation: Request the process flow for a representative part. A professional shop will show routing, tooling plan, inspection gates, and cycle time estimates.
8
Non-conformance handling: Ask how they handle defective parts. Look for a documented NCR (Non-Conformance Report) process with root cause analysis, corrective action, and customer notification.
9
Capacity and lead time reliability: Ask for their current utilization rate and typical delivery performance (on-time percentage). A shop running at 95%+ capacity may miss your deadlines.
10
Financial stability: For large orders ($50,000+), consider running a credit check through Dun & Bradstreet China or Sinosure. A supplier that goes bankrupt mid-order is a costly disaster.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • No factory photos or videos — Legitimate manufacturers are proud of their shop floors. Trading companies hide theirs.
  • Prices significantly below market — If a quote is 40%+ below other bids, the supplier is either cutting corners on material, using worn tooling, or planning to renegotiate after your deposit.
  • Unwillingness to provide material certificates — Any shop using certified material can provide mill test reports. Refusal is a red flag for material substitution.
  • No CMM or no CMM report — If they cannot provide a First Article Inspection (FAI) report with CMM data, they cannot verify they are making parts to your specifications.
  • Communication delays exceeding 24 hours — Responsiveness during the quoting phase predicts responsiveness during production. Slow communication before the order only gets worse after.
  • 100% prepayment requirement — Standard terms are 50/50 (deposit/balance). Demanding full prepayment on a first order signals either cash-flow problems or bad faith.

6. Quality Management: Inspection & QC

Quality management is where online CNC machining services in China differ most dramatically. The best suppliers operate quality systems comparable to European or Japanese standards. The worst rely on visual inspection and a hope that defects will not be noticed. Here is what to demand.

First Article Inspection (FAI)

For any new part, require a full FAI per AS9102 (even if your parts are not aerospace). A proper FAI includes:

  • Ballooned drawing with numbered dimensions
  • CMM measurement report for all critical dimensions
  • Material test report (MTR) with chemical composition and mechanical properties
  • Surface finish measurement (Ra/Rz) at specified locations
  • Hardness test results (if heat treatment is specified)
  • Photographs of the finished part from multiple angles
  • Any special test results (pressure test, conductivity, etc.)

A supplier who pushes back on FAI requirements is a supplier you should not use.

In-Process Inspection

Critical dimensions should be checked during production — not just at the end. Ask your supplier to define inspection gates:

  1. Incoming material inspection: Verify material certificates match the physical material (hardness check, PMI test)
  2. First-off inspection: Measure the first piece from each setup before running the batch
  3. In-process SPC: For production volumes above 500 pieces, statistical process control on critical dimensions
  4. Final inspection: 100% inspection on critical dimensions, AQL sampling on non-critical dimensions

Third-Party Inspection Services

For orders above $10,000 or safety-critical components, consider hiring a third-party inspection firm to verify parts at the supplier's facility before shipment:

  • SGS — Global leader, offices in every major Chinese manufacturing city
  • TUV Rheinland / TUV SUD — Strong in automotive and industrial components
  • Bureau Veritas — Comprehensive inspection and testing services
  • Asia Inspection (QIMA) — Cost-effective option for commercial components

Cost: $300–500 per man-day for a standard dimensional inspection. This is insurance against receiving a shipment of non-conforming parts — which would cost far more to rework or reject.

GD&T Communication

Miscommunication on geometric dimensioning and tolerancing is the single biggest quality risk when sourcing CNC machining services from China. Follow these rules:

  • Always provide 2D drawings with GD&T callouts — do not rely on the 3D model alone
  • Use ISO 1101 standard (not ASME Y14.5 unless you confirm the supplier is trained in it)
  • Specify datum references explicitly — do not assume the supplier will interpret them correctly
  • Call out surface finish requirements on the drawing (Ra values at specific surfaces)
  • Include a tolerance table on the drawing that specifies default tolerances for untoleranced dimensions (e.g., ISO 2768-m for general, ISO 2768-f for fine)
  • Flag critical-to-quality (CTQ) dimensions with a diamond symbol or note — these get 100% inspection

  • Section 301 tariff: Additional 7.5% or 25% depending on product classification (List 1–4A)

  • EU: Standard MFN duty of 0–5% for machined components. No additional punitive tariffs on CNC parts from China as of 2026.
  • India: Basic customs duty of 7.5–15% plus GST (18%) on imported machined components. Total landed cost markup: 25–35%.

Intellectual Property Protection

IP theft is a legitimate concern — but it is manageable with proper precautions:

  1. Use a bilingual NDA that is enforceable in Chinese courts. Have it drafted or reviewed by a Chinese IP lawyer. A Chinese-language NDA is far more enforceable than an English-only document.
  2. Register your patents and designs in China through CNIPA (China National Intellectual Property Administration). China operates on a first-to-register system — if your competitor registers first, they own the rights in China.
  3. Split manufacturing across suppliers so no single vendor sees the complete assembly. This is standard practice for sensitive military and medical products.
  4. Control your CAD data: Send STEP files (not native CAD with design history). Watermark drawings with the supplier's name and "Confidential" markings.
  5. Build long-term relationships: A supplier earning $500,000/year from your account has no incentive to risk that relationship by copying your design.

8. Communication & Project Management

Technical Drawing Standards

Clear technical communication prevents 80% of quality problems. Follow this protocol:

  • 3D model format: STEP (.stp) is the universal standard. Avoid IGES (older, less reliable geometry transfer). Do not send native CAD files unless specifically requested.
  • 2D drawing format: PDF for reference, DWG/DXF if the supplier needs to extract data. Include all GD&T, material callouts, surface finish requirements, and special notes on the 2D drawing.
  • Revision control: Use a clear revision numbering system (Rev A, Rev B, etc.) and always reference the revision number in every email and PO. Mismatched revisions are a leading cause of machining errors.
  • Units: Confirm whether drawings are in mm or inches. Most Chinese shops work in metric — sending inch-dimensioned drawings without a clear note invites conversion errors.

Time Zone Management

China operates on a single time zone (CST/UTC+8). Here is how the overlap works:

  • US East Coast (EST/UTC-5): 13-hour difference. Your 8 PM = their 9 AM next day. Morning emails from the US are answered by your afternoon.
  • US West Coast (PST/UTC-8): 16-hour difference. Send messages before 5 PM and expect replies by early morning.
  • India (IST/UTC+5:30): 2.5-hour difference. Excellent overlap — communicate in real time during normal business hours.
  • Central Europe (CET/UTC+1): 7-hour difference. Morning overlap is possible for urgent calls.

Best practice: Use WeChat or WhatsApp for quick questions (most Chinese engineers respond within 1–2 hours during business hours). Use email for formal communications, RFQs, and PO confirmations. Avoid scheduling weekly calls at inconvenient times — asynchronous communication works better for routine project management.

How much does CNC machining cost in China?
CNC machining shop rates in China range from $15 to $35 per hour depending on machine type, region, and supplier tier. Simple 3-axis milling starts at $15–22/hr, while 5-axis machining runs $30–45/hr. This compares to $75–150/hr in the US and $85–250/hr in Western Europe. The total landed cost savings (including shipping and duties) is typically 35–55%.
What is the minimum order quantity for CNC machining from China?
Most Chinese CNC machining suppliers accept orders as low as 1 piece for prototyping. However, economical production runs typically start at 50–100 pieces. Many online CNC machining services in China have no MOQ requirement, though per-part pricing decreases significantly at volumes above 100 units.
How long does it take to get CNC machined parts from China?
Production lead time is typically 5–10 business days for standard parts and 15–20 days for complex assemblies or mold components. Add 3–5 days for air freight or 25–35 days for sea freight to the US. Total door-to-door time: 10–15 days via air or 30–55 days via sea.
Is CNC machining quality from China reliable?
Quality varies significantly between suppliers. ISO 9001 certified shops with CMM inspection equipment consistently deliver parts to tight tolerances. The key is proper supplier vetting: request sample parts, audit their inspection equipment, and verify their quality management system before placing production orders. Top-tier Chinese CNC shops deliver quality comparable to US and European suppliers.
What certifications should a Chinese CNC machining supplier have?
At minimum, look for ISO 9001:2015. For automotive parts, IATF 16949. For aerospace, AS9100D. For medical devices, ISO 13485. Also verify that calibration certificates are current and traceable to national metrology standards (CNAS accredited).
What materials are commonly CNC machined in China?
Chinese CNC shops routinely machine aluminum alloys (6061, 7075, 2024), stainless steels (303, 304, 316L), tool steels (P20, H13, S136), carbon steels (1045, 4140), brass (C360), copper alloys, titanium (Ti-6Al-4V), and engineering plastics (PEEK, Delrin, Nylon, PTFE). Material sourcing within China is fast and cost-effective.
How do I protect my IP when outsourcing CNC machining to China?
Use a bilingual NDA (English + Chinese) enforceable under Chinese law, register your designs with CNIPA, split component manufacturing across multiple suppliers so no single vendor sees the full assembly, watermark all drawings, and build long-term supplier relationships that make IP theft unprofitable.
What is the best region in China for CNC machining?
Ningbo/Yuyao for mold tooling and automotive parts, Shenzhen/Dongguan for electronics and consumer products, and Shanghai/Suzhou for aerospace and medical precision machining. Each region has a distinct supply chain ecosystem optimized for different industries.
What tolerances can Chinese CNC shops achieve?
Standard tolerance: ±0.05mm. Precision machining: ±0.01mm. Swiss-type CNC turning (e.g., TSUGAMI M08J-II): ±0.005mm. Wire EDM: ±0.003mm. Always specify tolerances on your drawing — untoleranced dimensions default to ISO 2768-m.
How do tariffs affect the cost of CNC machining from China?
US Section 301 tariffs on CNC machined parts from China range from 7.5% to 25% depending on HTS classification. EU duties are typically 0–5%. India charges 7.5–15% basic customs duty. Even with maximum US tariffs, the landed cost advantage of Chinese CNC machining is typically 35–50%.
What file formats should I send for CNC machining quotes?
Send STEP (.stp/.step) files for 3D models — this is the universal standard. Include 2D drawings in PDF format with GD&T callouts, material specifications, surface finish requirements, and tolerances. Avoid sending native CAD formats unless your supplier specifically requests them.
Can Chinese CNC shops do surface finishing?
Yes. Common finishes include anodizing (Type II and Type III), electroless nickel plating, chrome plating, powder coating, bead blasting, polishing to Ra 0.05µm, passivation, black oxide, and painting. Most shops offer these in-house or through certified local partners within the same industrial zone.
What is the difference between a factory and a trading company?
A factory owns its own machines, employs machinists, and controls quality directly. A trading company acts as a middleman with a 15–30% markup. To verify: ask for a video tour of their shop floor, check their business license for 'manufacturing' classification, and confirm the factory address on satellite imagery.
How do payment terms work with Chinese CNC suppliers?
Standard: 50% deposit via T/T before production, 50% balance before shipment. For established relationships: 30/70 split or net-30 terms. For orders under $5,000, some suppliers accept PayPal. Large projects over $50,000 may use Letter of Credit. Avoid 100% prepayment on first orders.
What CNC machine brands indicate a quality Chinese supplier?
Top tier: Mazak, Okuma, Makino, DMG MORI, Hermle, TSUGAMI, Charmilles, Sodick. Good tier: Haas, Doosan, Brother, Tongtai. These brands represent significant capital investment and signal a supplier committed to precision. Ask for the machine list with models and years.
Should I use an online platform or a direct factory?
Online platforms (instant quoting, fast turnaround) are ideal for prototyping and small batches under 100 pieces. Direct factory relationships provide 20–40% lower pricing on production volumes, better customization, and dedicated engineering support. For annual spend above $10,000, a direct relationship is more cost-effective.
What inspection equipment should a Chinese CNC shop have?
Essential: CMM (Zeiss, Hexagon, or Mitutoyo), surface profilometer, hardness testers (Rockwell and Vickers), optical comparator or vision measuring system, and ISO 17025 calibrated hand tools. For mold work, also look for contour measurement and 3D scanning capability.
Can I visit the factory before ordering?
Yes, and reputable suppliers encourage it. Plan your visit to include: shop floor walk-through, inspection lab review, raw material storage check, and a meeting with the engineering team. If you cannot visit in person, request a live video tour or hire SGS, TUV, or Bureau Veritas to audit the factory on your behalf.
